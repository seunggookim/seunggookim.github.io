## Does music evoke emotions?
While the need of verification never personally occurred to me (like.. of course?!), all comes from the fundamental problem of psychology: 
_We cannot directly observe others' subjective experience._
It would be unsurprising that some music does not "tell you anything" (i.e., does not evoke any "meaningful" emotions other than boredom or disgust).
But, more surprisingly to some, a systematic survey found that some people just don't like music at all while these people were otherwise indifferent from those who enjoyed music [(Mas-Herrero et al., 2013)](https://doi.org/10.1525/mp.2013.31.2.118), which was followed by a large-scale survey and neural and physiological correlates. 
This particular disinterest in music was termed as "musical anhedonia" although whether this constitutes a clinical deficit to be listed in psychiatric manuals ushc as DSM-5 is unclear.
Nonetheless, this evoked many studies on individual variability in musical reward sensitivity.


## Does music evoke specific emotions? 
While acknowledging a great diversity in evoked emotions across individuals (and cultures) by an identical piece of music, it is still fascinating that such abstract sounds of music can evoke vivid and intense emotions.
But do we experience similar emotions from an identical piece of music?
If not, can we say it is music that evokes a certain emotion?

Or, is this not how affective causality works? 
For example, an audio-visual stimulus of a smiling baby👶 (or a baby animal🐶🐱) "generally" evokes a joyful emotion to many people.
But how reliable is this causation?
There are other factors that modulates the evocation of the joyful emotion.
But this does not mean the causation (or association?) is completely random[^1].

[^1]:Perhaps this may be even an absolutist confusion about empirical causation. An identical temperature may or may not boil the water depending on the pressure. Does it abolish the causal link between the temperature and the phase transition of the water?🙄
